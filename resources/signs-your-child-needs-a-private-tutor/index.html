5 Signs Your Child Needs a Private Tutor

Most parents wait too long. By the time the signs are obvious, the gap has been growing quietly for months. Here is what to look for before it becomes critical.

Ustadi Learning · 5 min read · April 2026

The Kenyan school system is built for the average pace. One teacher, forty students, one speed. If your child is keeping up — the system works well enough. If they are not — the gap grows quietly every single term until it becomes impossible to ignore.

The problem is that most parents only notice when the report card arrives. By then, three months of learning have passed. Three months of sitting in a classroom and nodding — without understanding.

Here are five signs to watch for before the report card tells you what you missed.

1. They study for a long time but their marks do not improve

This is the most overlooked sign. A parent sees a child with their books open for two hours every evening and assumes the work is being done. What they cannot see is whether the child actually understands what they are reading.

In the Kenyan context — where rote learning is common — a child can memorise notes perfectly and still fail an exam that asks them to apply the concept rather than repeat it. Long study hours with flat results is almost always a sign that the child is missing a foundational concept that a tutor can identify and fix in one session.

2. They are reluctant to discuss school subjects at home

When a child understands something, they talk about it. They explain it, demonstrate it, use it in conversation. When they do not understand — they avoid the topic entirely. Watch for the child who changes the subject when you ask how school went. Watch for the one-word answers about specific subjects.

This is not attitude. It is avoidance — and avoidance is always rooted in confusion or fear of being found out.

3. Their performance drops suddenly in one specific subject

Kenyan curricula — both CBC and KCSE — build on themselves. Each topic in Mathematics, for example, depends on understanding the previous one. When a child who was performing reasonably well suddenly drops in one subject — they almost always missed a foundational topic that everything subsequent is built on.

This is the easiest situation for a tutor to fix. Identify the gap. Fill the gap. The rest rebuilds quickly once the foundation is restored.

4. They say they understand in class but cannot do homework alone

This is extremely common and almost always misread by parents as laziness. What is actually happening is called passive understanding — the child follows along when the teacher explains but cannot reproduce the thinking independently.

It is the difference between watching someone swim and being able to swim yourself. A private tutor creates the conditions for active understanding — where the child must think through the problem themselves with guidance, not just watch someone else solve it.

5. KCSE or a major exam is approaching and the gaps are still there

Form 3 and Form 4 is when this becomes urgent. The exam is fixed. The syllabus is fixed. Time is the only variable. A parent who waits until mid-Form 4 to address a gap that has existed since Form 2 is working against time rather than with it.

The right time to bring in a tutor is not when the situation is critical. It is when you first notice something is not right. One term of targeted tutoring in Form 3 is worth three terms of panic in Form 4.

What to do if you recognise any of these signs

Do not wait for the next report card to confirm what you already suspect. The earlier the intervention, the smaller the gap that needs to be filled and the less pressure on your child before exams.
